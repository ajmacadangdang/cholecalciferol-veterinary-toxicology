What Is It — and What Do You Do?

Rodenticide Bait
Products such as Quintox® and Rampage® contain high-dose cholecalciferol. A single bait station contains sufficient toxin to cause lethal toxicosis in a medium-sized dog. Pets may ingest directly or via relay toxicosis from eating a poisoned rodent.

Vitamin D3 Supplements
Human vitamin D3 capsules, tablets, and drops — commonly found in homes. High-potency formulations (5,000–50,000 IU) pose the greatest risk. Accidental ingestion and miscalculated veterinary dosing are the most common iatrogenic causes.

Species at Risk

Dogs & Cats Most Affected
Dogs and cats are the primary species affected due to metabolic sensitivity to excess calcitriol. Cats may be slightly more sensitive. Unlike rodents, companion animals lack adaptive mechanisms to tolerate large vitamin D3 doses — the basis for its efficacy as a selective rodenticide.

Section 2

Mechanism of Toxicity

Cholecalciferol does not act directly — it hijacks the body's calcium regulation system through four simultaneous pathways. Click each node to reveal the mechanism.

⬇ Cholecalciferol ingested — absorbed from GI tract
Cholecalciferol (Vitamin D3) is absorbed from the GI tract and enters systemic circulation. At toxic doses, normal regulatory mechanisms that limit vitamin D activity are overwhelmed.
↓
🔬 Liver → 25-hydroxycholecalciferol  |  Kidney → 1,25-dihydroxycholecalciferol (calcitriol)
In the liver, cholecalciferol is converted to 25-hydroxycholecalciferol (calcidiol), then further hydroxylated in the kidney to the biologically active 1,25-dihydroxycholecalciferol (calcitriol). At toxic doses, calcitriol accumulates massively and drives severe hypercalcemia through four concurrent mechanisms.
↓
📈 Four simultaneous calcium-raising mechanisms — click to expand
Active calcitriol is a potent steroid hormone acting simultaneously on four anatomical targets. All four operate concurrently, which is why hypercalcemia is so rapid, severe, and difficult to reverse. Treatment must also target multiple pathways simultaneously.
↓
🍽️ ↑ GI Ca²⁺ absorption
🫘 ↓ Renal Ca²⁺ excretion
🧬 ↑ Calcium-binding protein (calbindin)
🦴 ↑ Bone Ca²⁺ mobilization (osteoclast activation)
Calcitriol dramatically upregulates calcium transporters (e.g., TRPV6, calbindin-D9k) in intestinal epithelium, causing massive absorption of dietary calcium from the gut — far beyond normal physiological limits.
Calcitriol stimulates renal tubular calcium reabsorption via upregulation of TRPV5 channels in the distal convoluted tubule, causing the kidney to retain calcium that would otherwise be eliminated.
Calcitriol upregulates synthesis of calbindin (calcium-binding proteins), which amplify intracellular calcium transport capacity in enterocytes and renal tubular cells, further driving calcium accumulation.
Calcitriol activates osteoclasts which resorb bone mineral matrix, releasing stored calcium into the bloodstream. This is particularly significant in prolonged or severe toxicosis where skeletal reserves contribute meaningfully to hypercalcemia.
↓
⚠️ Severe hypercalcemia → Ca × P >70 mg/L → Soft tissue mineralization & organ failure
Serum calcium climbs dangerously. When the calcium × phosphorus product exceeds 70 mg/L, calcium salts precipitate in soft tissues. Target organs: heart, kidneys, blood vessels, lungs, stomach wall, and pancreas. Cardiac mineralization causes arrhythmia; renal mineralization causes AKI. Once established, soft tissue mineralization may be irreversible.

Neurological / General
CNS & General Signs
◆Anorexia — often the earliest presenting sign
◆Weakness and lethargy — progressive with rising calcium
◆Depression / obtundation — reflects hypercalcemia's effect on neuromuscular function
Non-specific signs make early diagnosis challenging without exposure history.
Cardiovascular
Heart & Circulation
◆Bradycardia — slowed conduction due to excess Ca²⁺ effects on cardiac action potentials
◆Ventricular arrhythmia — potentially life-threatening
◆Hypertension — from vascular smooth muscle dysfunction
Cardiac mineralization can cause permanent myocardial damage.
Gastrointestinal
GI Tract Signs
◆Vomiting — from GI irritation and direct hypercalcemia effects
◆Diarrhea — often with blood
◆Melena (dark tarry feces) — indicates GI ulceration and intestinal bleeding
GI ulceration occurs secondary to hypercalcemia and requires specific treatment.
Renal
Kidney & Urinary Signs
◆Polyuria (PU) — kidneys unable to concentrate urine due to tubular dysfunction
◆Polydipsia (PD) — compensatory increased water intake
◆Calciuria — calcium spillage into urine
◆Low urine specific gravity — isosthenuria from tubular damage
Hypercalcemia causes nephrogenic diabetes insipidus-like syndrome and direct tubular injury.
Systemic / Tissues
Soft Tissue Mineralization
◆Occurs when Ca × P product exceeds 70 mg/L
◆Target organs: heart, pancreas, kidney, lung, stomach wall
◆Confirmed histologically at necropsy or biopsy
Once established, soft tissue mineralization may be irreversible. Prevention requires early intervention.
⏱️ Key timing point: All these signs emerge 36–48 hours after ingestion — the same timeline as Buddy's presentation. The delay reflects the time required for two-step metabolic activation of cholecalciferol to calcitriol, and the subsequent calcium accumulation to pathological levels.

⚠ Critical Values Present
📈 Serum Phosphorus Rises before calcium — key early marker 9.1 mg/dL ↑ tap ▾
Rises first — earliest detectable lab abnormality. Critical threshold is >8 mg/dL. Phosphorus elevation precedes the calcium rise in cholecalciferol toxicosis — this temporal sequence is diagnostically important and helps distinguish this toxicosis from other causes of hypercalcemia.
🔴 Total Calcium Normal canine range: 9.0–11.3 mg/dL 15.2 mg/dL ↑↑ tap ▾
Critically elevated — above the 13 mg/dL diagnostic threshold. Calculate: Ca × P = 15.2 × 9.1 = 138.3 mg/L — nearly double the critical mineralization threshold of 70 mg/L. Urgent treatment required.
🔬 Parathyroid Hormone (PTH) Critical differentiating finding Low / Suppressed tap ▾
Suppressed PTH with high calcium is a key differentiator. The parathyroid gland appropriately suppresses PTH in response to elevated serum calcium. This distinguishes cholecalciferol toxicosis from primary hyperparathyroidism (elevated PTH) and helps rule out paraneoplastic disease (elevated PTHrP, not PTH).
🫘 BUN & Creatinine Renal function markers Both Elevated tap ▾
Azotemia confirms acute kidney injury (AKI). Calcium deposition in renal tubules impairs glomerular filtration and tubular function. Serial monitoring every 12–24 hours is essential. IV fluid diuresis is urgently needed to protect remaining renal function.
💧 Urine Specific Gravity With calciuria present Low — Isosthenuria tap ▾
Kidneys unable to concentrate urine — tubular dysfunction confirmed. Low specific gravity (~1.008–1.012) reflects hypercalcemia-induced tubular injury. Calciuria — calcium in the urine — is also present, providing direct evidence of calcium overflow into the urinary tract.
☀️ 25-Hydroxycholecalciferol Measurable in bile and kidney tissue Elevated tap ▾
Confirmatory finding — definitive when exposure history is uncertain. Elevated 25-hydroxycholecalciferol and its active metabolite in bile and kidney tissue confirm the diagnosis definitively. Not always immediately available — clinical diagnosis and treatment must not be delayed pending this result.

Other Differentials — Click to Expand
Key Differentiator
Caused by PTHrP secreted by tumors (lymphoma is most common in dogs). PTHrP mimics PTH activity but is detected separately on assay panels.

How to distinguish: PTHrP is elevated (not PTH itself). Look for lymphadenopathy, splenomegaly, or abnormal cytology. No history of toxin exposure. Thoracic and abdominal imaging is essential in any hypercalcemic dog where toxin exposure cannot be confirmed.
Key Differentiator
Caused by a functional parathyroid adenoma or hyperplasia. The gland fails to suppress PTH despite high serum calcium — PTH is inappropriately elevated.

How to distinguish: PTH is elevated or inappropriately normal despite hypercalcemia — the opposite of cholecalciferol toxicosis. Cervical ultrasound may identify a parathyroid mass. No toxin exposure history.
Key Differentiator
Seen particularly in young Labrador Retrievers — Buddy's breed. Mechanism is incompletely understood; possibly related to PTHrP or dietary calcium/phosphorus handling.

How to distinguish: Diagnosis of exclusion — rule out toxin exposure, neoplasia, and primary hyperparathyroidism first. Buddy's breed makes this a genuine differential alongside the toxicosis history. Responds to dietary calcium restriction.
Key Differentiator
Ethylene glycol is metabolised to oxalic acid, which forms calcium oxalate monohydrate crystals in renal tubules — a pathognomonic finding on urine microscopy.

• Kidney Ca: >8,000 ppm (vs. 2,000–3,000 with cholecalciferol)
• Ca:P ratio in kidney: >2.5 (vs. 0.4–0.7)
• Calcium oxalate crystals on urine microscopy — pathognomonic
• History of access to antifreeze (ethylene glycol-containing products)
Section 6

Treating Cholecalciferol Toxicity

Treatment must be aggressive, multi-modal, and sustained over weeks. Click each treatment card to reveal mechanism, dose, and clinical notes.

⏰
GI Decontamination Window — Critical Timing
Emesis induction and activated charcoal are only effective within 6–8 hours of ingestion. Beyond this window, cholecalciferol is absorbed and metabolic activation to calcitriol is underway. At 40 hours, this window is closed — proceed directly to supportive care.
💉
IV Normal Saline
0.9% NaCl — aggressive fluid diuresis
tap to expand ▾
Mechanism: Expands extracellular volume, increases GFR, and reduces tubular reabsorption of calcium. Restores renal perfusion in AKI secondary to hypercalcemia.
First-line treatment. Begin immediately. Used in combination with furosemide for maximum calcium excretion. Monitor urine output closely.
🌊
Furosemide
Loop diuretic — promotes Ca²⁺ excretion
tap to expand ▾
Mechanism: Inhibits the Na-K-2Cl cotransporter in the thick ascending loop of Henle, increasing urinary calcium excretion. Works synergistically with IV saline diuresis.
⚠ Never use furosemide alone. Without concurrent IV fluids, furosemide worsens dehydration and can paradoxically concentrate serum calcium, accelerating mineralization.
🛡️
Prednisolone
2–6 mg/kg/day PO or IV
tap to expand ▾
Dual mechanism: (1) Reduces intestinal calcium absorption by antagonizing calcitriol effects on GI epithelium; (2) Reduces bone and renal calcium resorption by blocking calcitriol-mediated osteoclast activation.
Continue for weeks — do not stop early. Cholecalciferol is fat-soluble with a prolonged half-life. Premature discontinuation is a common cause of rebound hypercalcemia.
💎
Pamidronate (Aredia)
1.3–2 mg/kg IV over 2–4 hours
tap to expand ▾
Mechanism: Bisphosphonate that binds to hydroxyapatite in bone and inhibits osteoclast-mediated bone resorption, blocking calcium mobilization from skeletal stores. Produces a potent, sustained effect. Has replaced calcitonin as the preferred agent.
⚠ Cost: ~$300 per dose. Discuss with owners before administering. Despite the cost, pamidronate is often the most effective single intervention for severe hypercalcemia.
🦴
Bisphosphonates (oral)
Alendronate — ongoing outpatient management
tap to expand ▾
Mechanism: Same class as pamidronate — inhibit osteoclast-mediated bone resorption. Oral options allow continued outpatient management after initial IV treatment.
Alendronate (~10 mg/dog/day) is a practical oral option for home management. Continue alongside prednisolone taper for several weeks after discharge.
🩹
Sucralfate / Milk of Magnesia
Sucralfate 0.25–1 g PO q6–8h
tap to expand ▾
Dual benefit: (1) Sucralfate forms a protective barrier over GI ulceration caused by hypercalcemia-induced mucosal damage; (2) Milk of magnesia (magnesium hydroxide) binds phosphate in the gut, reducing intestinal phosphorus absorption and lowering the Ca × P product.
Consider adding omeprazole for additional acid suppression in patients with significant melena or hematemesis.
🥗
Dietary Modification
Low-calcium, low-phosphorus prescription diet
tap to expand ▾
Mechanism: Reduces exogenous calcium and phosphorus load while calcitriol is driving maximal absorption. Lowering dietary phosphorus directly reduces the Ca × P product and mineralization risk.
Continue dietary restriction for weeks to months given the fat-soluble, long-acting nature of stored cholecalciferol. Avoid dairy products and calcium supplements.
📊
Monitoring Protocol
Serial labs every 12–24h during hospitalization
tap to expand ▾
Monitor: total and ionized calcium, phosphorus (Ca × P product), BUN and creatinine, urine output and specific gravity, ECG for arrhythmia, and blood pressure.
Treatment endpoint: Total calcium 9–11 mg/dL, Ca × P below 70 mg/L consistently, renal values trending normal, stable cardiac rhythm. Do not discharge until calcium is stable for ≥24h.

Rebound hypercalcemia is a common complication when prednisolone or bisphosphonates are discontinued prematurely. Plan for a minimum of 4–6 weeks of continued management post-discharge.
